This snippet has a few real problems, the most likely bug being an off-by-one in the offset math. Here's what stands out, roughly in order of severity:

**1. No validation — `NaN` flows into the query.** If `page` is missing or non-numeric (`?page=abc`, or no `page` at all), `parseInt` returns `NaN`, so `offset = NaN * 20 = NaN`. That `NaN` then goes into your `LIMIT/OFFSET` and either errors or behaves unpredictably depending on the driver.

**2. Likely off-by-one.** `offset = page * 20` is 0-indexed. If your API contract is 1-indexed (the common case — `?page=1` means the first page), then `page=1` produces `offset=20` and silently skips the first 20 rows. You probably want `(page - 1) * 20`.

**3. No floor for negative/huge values.** `?page=-5` yields a negative offset (error in most databases). There's also no upper bound, so deep pagination (`?page=99999999`) is a cheap way to hammer the DB.

**4. Missing radix.** `parseInt(x, 10)` is the safe habit; rely on the default and a leading-zero or unusual input can bite you.

**5. `req.query.page` may be an array.** Express turns `?page=1&page=2` into `['1','2']`; `parseInt` then coerces it to the string `"1,2"` and parses `1`. Not a crash, but not what you intended either.

A version that closes 1–4:

```js
const page = Math.max(1, parseInt(req.query.page, 10) || 1);
const offset = (page - 1) * 20;
```
