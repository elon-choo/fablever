For a side project, default to a platform like Render or Fly. The scarce resource is your attention, and a platform buys back nearly all the operational time a VPS would consume: git-push deploys, automatic HTTPS, managed Postgres, log streaming, and zero OS patching or firewall babysitting. You get to spend your evenings on the project instead of on the server.

Reach for a plain VPS (Hetzner, DigitalOcean, etc.) only if one of these is actually true for you:

- **You want to learn ops.** Running your own box is the best way to actually understand Linux, nginx/Caddy, systemd, and TLS. If that's a goal, the time cost becomes the point.
- **You're running several services and want a flat, cheap bill.** A €5–6/mo Hetzner box can host a handful of small apps. Platform pricing is great at zero and creeps up once you have multiple always-on services or a database.
- **You need something platforms make awkward** — long-running background workers, persistent local disk, cron at the OS level, unusual networking, or a GPU.

The cost picture in practice: platforms are cheapest at tiny scale (often free, modulo cold starts on idle free tiers) and a VPS wins on predictable always-on cost. Neither difference is large enough to matter for a side project's budget — so decide on time and control, not dollars.

My concrete pick: start on Fly or Render, ship the thing, and only graduate to a VPS if you hit a real limitation or you specifically want the sysadmin practice. It's far easier to outgrow a platform and move down to a VPS than to lose momentum fighting infrastructure before your project is even live.
