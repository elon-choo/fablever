HTTPS is just HTTP running inside a TLS tunnel. TLS gives you three guarantees at once — **confidentiality** (eavesdroppers see only ciphertext), **integrity** (tampering is detected), and **authentication** (you're really talking to the server named in the URL, not an impostor). Here's how it actually delivers them.

## The core trick: asymmetric crypto to bootstrap symmetric crypto

Public-key cryptography (RSA, or more commonly elliptic-curve today) is slow but lets two strangers agree on a secret over an open wire. Symmetric crypto (AES, ChaCha20) is fast but needs both sides to already share a key. TLS uses the slow method *once*, at the start, to establish a shared secret — then switches to the fast method for the actual data. The handshake is the part that does this.

## The handshake (TLS 1.3, the modern version)

1. **ClientHello** — your browser says hello, lists the cipher suites and TLS versions it supports, and (this is the 1.3 speedup) immediately throws in its **key-share**: an ephemeral Diffie-Hellman public value.

2. **ServerHello** — the server picks a cipher suite, sends back *its* ephemeral key-share, and includes its **certificate** plus a signature. From the two key-shares, both sides independently compute the same shared secret via Diffie-Hellman — and crucially, that secret never crosses the wire. An attacker recording everything still can't derive it.

3. **Key derivation** — both sides run the shared secret through a key-derivation function (HKDF) to produce the symmetric session keys. Everything after this point is AES/ChaCha20-encrypted.

TLS 1.3 finishes this in **one round trip** (1-RTT), versus two in TLS 1.2. It also dropped the old RSA key-transport mode entirely, which matters for the next point.

## Why each guarantee holds

**Confidentiality** comes from the symmetric cipher using keys that were never transmitted. Modern TLS uses **AEAD** ciphers (e.g. AES-GCM, ChaCha20-Poly1305) which encrypt and authenticate in one operation.

**Integrity** comes from the AEAD authentication tag attached to every record. Flip a single bit in transit and the tag fails to verify — the connection aborts rather than delivering corrupted or injected data.

**Authentication** is the part people underestimate, and it's what stops a man-in-the-middle. Diffie-Hellman alone protects you from a *passive* eavesdropper but not an *active* attacker who sits in the middle and does two separate handshakes. The certificate closes that hole:

- The server's certificate contains its public key and domain name, signed by a **Certificate Authority** (CA).
- Your browser/OS ships with a trusted **root store** of CA public keys. It walks the **chain** from the server's cert up through intermediate CAs to a trusted root, checking each signature.
- It verifies the cert's domain matches the URL, that it hasn't expired, and that it isn't revoked (OCSP/CRL).
- Then the server **proves it owns the private key** by signing the handshake transcript. A MITM can copy the real cert but can't produce this signature without the private key — so the impersonation fails right there.

## Forward secrecy

Because the Diffie-Hellman keys are **ephemeral** — fresh per session, then discarded — even if the server's long-term private key is stolen *later*, an attacker who recorded past traffic still can't decrypt it. That property is **forward secrecy**, and it's why TLS 1.3 made ephemeral key exchange mandatory.

## The mental model

Certificates answer "who am I talking to," Diffie-Hellman answers "how do we get a shared secret past eavesdroppers," and the symmetric AEAD cipher answers "how do we move data fast while keeping it private and tamper-evident." The handshake's job is to chain those together so that by the time your first byte of real HTTP goes out, all three are already locked in.

One common misconception worth flagging: HTTPS secures data **in transit only**. It says nothing about whether the server stores your data safely, and it doesn't hide *which* site you're visiting — the domain leaks via DNS and the TLS SNI field (unless Encrypted Client Hello is in use), and traffic size/timing can still reveal patterns.
