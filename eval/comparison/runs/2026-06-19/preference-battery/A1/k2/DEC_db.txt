Postgres, for an analytics dashboard with heavy aggregate reads. It's the better fit on almost every axis that matters here, and MongoDB only pulls ahead in cases your description doesn't suggest.

Why Postgres wins for this workload:

- **Aggregation is its home turf.** `GROUP BY`, window functions, `ROLLUP`/`CUBE`, CTEs, and multi-table JOINs are first-class and the query planner is built to optimize them. MongoDB's aggregation pipeline can do a lot, but multi-collection joins (`$lookup`) and complex grouping get awkward and slow compared to SQL.
- **Tooling ecosystem.** Every BI/dashboard tool (Metabase, Superset, Looker, Grafana, Tableau) speaks SQL natively. You'll spend less time fighting connectors.
- **Scales into real OLAP cheaply.** When plain Postgres starts to strain, you have a clean upgrade path without leaving the ecosystem: TimescaleDB for time-series, Citus for distributed/columnar, materialized views for precomputed rollups, and partitioning for large fact tables.
- **Materialized views** specifically are a big lever for dashboards — precompute the expensive aggregates, refresh on a schedule, serve reads instantly.

When MongoDB would actually be the right call:

- Your data is genuinely schemaless or deeply nested per-document and varies a lot between records.
- You're write-heavy on semi-structured documents and reads are mostly single-document lookups, not cross-cutting aggregates.
- You're already storing event blobs as documents and aggregation is secondary.

Your stated profile — "heavy aggregate reads" — points squarely at the relational/columnar side, so Postgres.

One thing worth deciding early: if this is high-volume time-series/event data (millions of rows, time-bucketed queries), start with **Postgres + TimescaleDB** rather than vanilla Postgres. If it's moderate volume with mixed dimensions, vanilla Postgres with good indexing and materialized views is plenty.
