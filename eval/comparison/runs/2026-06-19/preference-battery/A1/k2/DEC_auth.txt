Use Auth0 (or an equivalent managed provider). For a small startup, rolling your own auth is almost always the wrong place to spend your limited engineering time.

The core reason: authentication looks simple — email, password, done — but the long tail is enormous and every item in it is security-critical. Password hashing and rotation, secure session/token handling, email verification, password reset flows, rate limiting and brute-force protection, account lockout, breach detection, MFA, OAuth social logins, and eventually SSO/SAML when you sell to your first business customer. Each of these is a place to get it subtly wrong, and getting auth wrong is the kind of bug that leaks your entire user base rather than breaking one feature.

When a managed provider wins (your situation):
- You're small and every hour spent on auth plumbing is an hour not spent on the product that differentiates you.
- You'll want social login, MFA, and SSO eventually — providers give you these as config rather than projects.
- The free/cheap tiers cover early-stage volume, so cost isn't a real factor yet.

When writing your own is defensible:
- Auth is genuinely core to your product (you *are* an identity company).
- You have specific requirements no provider supports, or hard data-residency/compliance constraints that forbid a third party holding credentials.
- You have in-house security expertise and the appetite to own it forever — it's not a one-time build, it's permanent maintenance.

Two practical notes on the decision:

Vendor lock-in is the real tradeoff to weigh, not build-vs-buy difficulty. Migrating off an auth provider later is painful because user identities and password hashes are entangled with it. Mitigate by keeping your own `users` table as the source of truth and treating the provider as the authentication layer on top — store your own user IDs and map provider identities to them.

Auth0 specifically is solid but gets expensive as you scale, and its pricing tiers have sharp jumps. Worth comparing against alternatives before committing: Clerk and Supabase Auth are popular for startups (Clerk especially if you want polished pre-built UI components), AWS Cognito if you're already on AWS and want lower cost at the expense of developer experience, and self-hostable options like Keycloak or Ory if you want managed-style features without the per-user SaaS bill.

My recommendation: start with a managed provider, keep your own user table as the system of record, and revisit only if pricing or a hard requirement forces it. The default for a small team should be to buy this, not build it.
