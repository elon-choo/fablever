The core move with a flaky test is to stop treating "flaky" as the diagnosis and turn it into a reproducible failure before you touch anything. Don't add a retry or `sleep` to make CI green — that hides the bug and it'll resurface. Here's the order I'd work in:

1. **Confirm it's the test, not the code.** A flaky test sometimes signals a real race or ordering bug in the product. Decide that first, because it changes whether the fix lives in the test or the source.

2. **Make it fail on demand.** Run it in a loop locally until it fails — `for i in {1..100}; do ...; done`, or your runner's repeat flag (`pytest-repeat`, `go test -count`, `jest --runInBand`). If it only fails in CI, the *environment* is the clue: CI is usually slower, more parallel, and differently ordered than your machine.

3. **Identify which flakiness class it is.** Most flaky tests are one of: a timing/async race (waiting on a fixed delay instead of a condition), shared state leaking between tests (DB rows, globals, temp files, test order dependence), non-determinism (clock, random seed, map/set iteration order), or an external dependency (network, real time). The reproduction conditions usually point straight at the class.

4. **Fix the root cause for that class.** Wait on conditions not durations; isolate or reset shared state in setup/teardown; inject the clock and seed; stub the external call. 

5. **Prove it.** Re-run the same loop that reliably failed and show it passing across many iterations — that's the evidence the fix worked, not "it looks right now."

If you point me at the test (file, framework, and the CI failure output if you have it), I'll dig into the specific cause rather than keep it general — that's usually faster than working from the category alone.
