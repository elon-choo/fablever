For a small startup: **use a managed auth provider, don't roll your own.** That's the default, and you'd need a specific reason to deviate.

## Why not roll your own

Auth looks simple (email + password, right?) but the real scope is large and unforgiving:

- Password hashing, reset flows, email verification, session/token management, rate limiting, account lockout
- OAuth/social login, SSO (SAML/OIDC) the moment you sell to a business
- MFA, device management, audit logs
- Staying current on attacks (credential stuffing, token theft, session fixation)

A bug here isn't a feature regression — it's a breach. As a small team, every hour spent on auth is an hour not spent on the thing that actually differentiates you. Auth is undifferentiated heavy lifting.

## But Auth0 specifically?

Auth0 is solid but **gets expensive fast** as you scale, and pricing has a history of jumps at the tier boundaries. Before defaulting to it, look at the alternatives — the landscape is much better than it was a few years ago:

| Option | Good fit when |
|---|---|
| **Clerk** | You want the fastest path, great React/Next.js DX, prebuilt UI components |
| **Supabase Auth** | You're already using Postgres/Supabase; cheap, you own the user data |
| **WorkOS** | You're B2B and SSO/SAML/SCIM for enterprise customers is the priority |
| **Auth0/Okta** | You need maturity, breadth of features, compliance certs out of the box |
| **AWS Cognito** | You're deep in AWS and willing to trade DX for cost |

## My recommendation

1. **Use a provider.** The custom-auth math almost never works out for a small startup.
2. **Pick based on your stack and customer type**, not brand name. B2C consumer app → Clerk or Supabase. Selling to businesses → WorkOS or Auth0. Already on Supabase → its built-in auth.
3. **Check the lock-in / export story** before committing — can you export users and password hashes if you leave? This is the main risk of going managed, and it's how you avoid being trapped when pricing changes.

The one legitimate reason to build your own is if auth *is* your product (you're an identity company) or you have hard regulatory/data-residency constraints no vendor meets. For a typical small startup, neither applies.
