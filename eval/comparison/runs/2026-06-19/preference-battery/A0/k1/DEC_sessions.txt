For a small app, **use the database** — don't add Redis.

## Why

The default should be the database you already have. Reach for Redis only when you hit a concrete reason to, and a small app usually won't.

**Database sessions win for a small app because:**
- **One less moving part.** Redis is another service to deploy, monitor, secure, back up, and pay for. For a small app that operational cost outweighs the benefit.
- **Sessions survive restarts** for free. A plain Redis instance loses everything on restart unless you configure persistence (RDB/AOF).
- **Transactional consistency.** Session state lives next to your user/auth data, so you can reason about it in one place.
- **Simplicity of querying.** "Show all active sessions for this user," forced logout, auditing — trivial SQL, awkward in Redis.

**The usual argument for Redis is speed and TTL**, but at small scale a session lookup is a single indexed primary-key read — a few hundred microseconds. You won't notice the difference until you're at meaningful traffic.

## When to switch to Redis later

Move sessions to Redis when you actually hit one of these:
- Session reads become a measurable hot path (thousands/sec) and show up in DB load.
- You scale to multiple app servers and want a fast shared store with automatic TTL eviction.
- You're already running Redis for caching/queues anyway — then colocating sessions there is reasonable.

## Even simpler option to consider

If your sessions are small and you don't need server-side revocation, **stateless signed cookies / JWTs** skip the storage question entirely. The tradeoff: you can't easily invalidate a session before it expires. If instant logout/revocation matters, stick with DB-backed sessions.

**Bottom line:** Start with the database. Add Redis when traffic — not speculation — tells you to.
