Pool exhaustion is almost always a symptom, not the disease. The instinct is to bump the pool size, but that usually just delays the same wall. Check these in order â€” cheapest and most common first.

## 1. Are connections being leaked or held too long? (most common)
A connection borrowed and never returned will exhaust any pool. Look for:
- **Code paths that acquire a connection but don't release on the error path** â€” a query throws, the `finally`/`using`/`defer release` is missing, connection never goes back.
- **Transactions held open across slow work** â€” e.g. you `BEGIN`, then make an HTTP call or do CPU work, *then* commit. The connection is pinned the whole time.
- **Connections borrowed for the lifetime of a request** instead of per-query.

Query to see what your live connections are actually doing right now:
```sql
SELECT pid, state, wait_event_type, wait_event,
       now() - state_change AS in_state,
       now() - xact_start  AS xact_age,
       left(query, 80) AS query
FROM pg_stat_activity
WHERE datname = current_database()
ORDER BY xact_age DESC NULLS LAST;
```
The tells: lots of `idle in transaction` â†’ you're holding transactions open (app bug). Lots of `active` with long `in_state` â†’ slow queries are the bottleneck. Lots of plain `idle` near your pool max â†’ connections borrowed and parked.

## 2. Is throughput just exceeding capacity? (Little's Law)
`required connections â‰ˆ request_rate Ă— avg_query_duration`. If queries that took 5ms now take 200ms under load, your effective capacity dropped 40Ă—, and the pool drains even with no leak. So check **whether query latency is climbing under load** â€” slow queries are the usual root cause that *presents* as pool exhaustion.
```sql
SELECT mean_exec_time, calls, left(query,80)
FROM pg_stat_statements
ORDER BY mean_exec_time * calls DESC LIMIT 20;
```

## 3. Pool sizing math
- **`pool_size Ă— app_instances` must stay under Postgres `max_connections`** (minus a reserve for superuser/migrations). A common failure: 10 app pods Ă— 20-conn pools = 200 connections against a `max_connections` of 100. Confirm: `SHOW max_connections;` vs. your total configured pool across all instances.
- Bigger isn't better. Past ~`(2 Ă— cores) + effective_spindles`, more connections *reduce* throughput (lock/CPU contention), making latency worse and exhaustion faster.

## 4. Pool timeouts & queue behavior
- What's your **acquire/checkout timeout**? If it's high, requests pile up silently waiting; if low, you get the "pool exhausted" error fast. The error is the messenger.
- Is **min/idle pool** so low that you pay connection-establishment cost on every spike?
