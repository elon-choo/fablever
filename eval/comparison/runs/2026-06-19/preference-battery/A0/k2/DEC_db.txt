For heavy aggregate reads, **Postgres is the better default** between the two. Here's the reasoning, then the caveat that actually matters.

## Why Postgres wins for this workload

- **Aggregation is SQL's home turf.** `GROUP BY`, window functions, `GROUPING SETS`/`ROLLUP`/`CUBE`, multi-table joins across a star schema — Postgres's query planner is far more mature here than MongoDB's aggregation pipeline.
- **Materialized views** let you precompute expensive rollups and refresh them on a schedule — often the single biggest win for a dashboard.
- **Indexing that fits analytics**: BRIN indexes are tiny and fast on time-ordered data (typical for dashboards), plus partial/expression indexes for common filters.
- **Partitioning** by time range keeps scans bounded as data grows.
- **JSONB** covers most of MongoDB's flexible-schema advantage when your events have varied shapes — you get document flexibility *and* relational aggregation in one engine.
- **Extensions** extend it directly into analytics territory: **TimescaleDB** (time-series, continuous aggregates) and **Citus** (distributed/columnar) if you outgrow a single node.

MongoDB is the stronger pick mainly when ingestion is write-heavy with genuinely document-shaped, deeply nested, schema-varying data — and your reads are mostly point/document lookups rather than wide aggregations. That's not what "heavy aggregate reads" describes.

## The caveat worth pausing on

If aggregate reads are the *dominant, defining* workload and data volume is large, **a purpose-built columnar/OLAP engine beats both row-stores** — ClickHouse, DuckDB (embedded/single-node), or a warehouse like BigQuery/Snowflake/Redshift. They store data by column, so scanning one metric across billions of rows touches a fraction of the data. Postgres-with-Citus-columnar or TimescaleDB narrows that gap without leaving the Postgres ecosystem.

## Practical recommendation

- **Starting out / moderate scale (≤ low hundreds of millions of rows):** Postgres, with materialized views + partitioning + BRIN indexes. Simplest operationally, one system to run.
- **Time-series-heavy dashboard:** Postgres + TimescaleDB (continuous aggregates are purpose-built for exactly this).
- **Scan-dominated at large scale:** add or migrate the analytics layer to ClickHouse/DuckDB, often keeping Postgres as the transactional source of truth.
