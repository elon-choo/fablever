For 3 related services, **default to a monorepo.** That's the right call for most teams, and "related" is the key word — it's doing the work in that sentence.

## Why monorepo wins here

- **Atomic cross-service changes.** Change a shared type/API contract and update all 3 consumers in one commit + one PR. With separate repos, that's a coordinated dance across 3 PRs with version-bump lag in between.
- **One source of truth for shared code.** Types, validation, client SDKs, config live in one place — no publishing internal packages to a registry just so service B can import from service A.
- **Single setup.** One clone, one dependency install, one lint/test/CI config. New contributors are productive immediately.
- **Refactoring stays honest.** Rename something and the compiler/tests catch every broken caller across all 3 services at once.

## When to split into separate repos instead

Pick separate repos if **two or more** of these are true:

- **Different teams own different services** with little overlap, and you want hard ownership/access boundaries (e.g. one service has stricter security/compliance scope).
- **Independent release cadences** that you don't *want* coupled — different deploy pipelines, on-call, versioning.
- **Open-sourcing one** service but not the others.
- **Radically different tech stacks** with no shared code at all (then the monorepo's main benefit evaporates).

## The nuance people miss

Monorepo ≠ monolith and ≠ shared deploy. You can keep a monorepo and still deploy each service independently — path-based CI triggers (only build/test/deploy what changed) give you that. So you don't sacrifice independent deployability by choosing a monorepo; you only need tooling for it once the repo gets large (Turborepo/Nx for JS, Bazel/Pants for polyglot, or just workspaces + path filters to start).

---

**My recommendation:** start with a monorepo using your ecosystem's native workspaces (npm/pnpm/yarn workspaces, Go modules, Cargo workspaces, etc.) and path-filtered CI. It's reversible — splitting a monorepo later is straightforward, while merging 3 repos with divergent histories and tooling is painful. Only reach for separate repos if the team-boundary or compliance reasons above clearly apply.
