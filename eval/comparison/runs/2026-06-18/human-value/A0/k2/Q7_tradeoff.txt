**Don't roll your own auth.** For a small startup, use a managed provider. The reasoning is pretty one-sided:

**Why not DIY**
- Auth is a huge, unforgiving surface area: password hashing, session/token management, email verification, password reset flows, MFA, social/OAuth logins, rate limiting, account lockout, refresh token rotation. Each is a place where a subtle mistake = a breach.
- It's the worst possible use of scarce early-stage eng time. It's pure undifferentiated plumbing — no customer pays you for it, but they'll leave you over a leak.
- "Just store a hashed password and a session cookie" balloons the moment you need social login, SSO, or compliance.

The only real case for owning more of it: hard regulatory/data-residency constraints, or auth *is* your product.

**Auth0 vs. the alternatives**

Auth0 is mature and reliable, but its pricing scales aggressively — the jump from free → paid tiers and especially B2B/enterprise (SSO, RBAC) features gets expensive fast, and it carries real vendor lock-in. It's a safe default, not the cheapest or the best-DX one.

Worth comparing before committing, depending on your stack:

| Need | Good fit |
|---|---|
| React/Next.js, great DX, fast start | **Clerk** |
| Already using Postgres / want DB + auth together | **Supabase Auth** |
| B2B selling to enterprises (SSO/SCIM is the point) | **WorkOS** |
| Want to self-host / avoid lock-in, own the data | **Better Auth**, Ory, or Keycloak |
| Deep in AWS | **Cognito** (cheap, rougher DX) |

**My recommendation:** Pick a managed provider that matches your stack rather than Auth0 by default. If you're on Next.js/React, **Clerk** or **Supabase Auth** will get you live faster and cheaper. Reserve Auth0/WorkOS for when enterprise SSO becomes a sales requirement. Keep the integration thin (wrap it behind your own small interface) so switching later is cheap.
