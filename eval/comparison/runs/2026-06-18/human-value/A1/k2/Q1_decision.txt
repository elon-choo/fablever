Start with REST. For most mobile backends it's the right default, and you should only reach for GraphQL if you hit specific conditions that REST handles poorly.

Here's the reasoning, and the honest caveat that it depends on your app's shape.

**Why REST is the better default for you**

- HTTP caching just works — CDNs, ETags, and client-side caching all key off URLs and standard headers. On mobile, where networks are flaky, this is a real win you get for free. GraphQL sends everything as `POST` to one endpoint, so you lose all of that and have to rebuild caching yourself (or adopt Apollo/Relay client caches).
- It's simpler to build, debug, and operate. You can hit an endpoint with `curl`, read logs by route, rate-limit per path, and onboard developers faster. GraphQL adds a schema, resolvers, and the N+1 query problem you'll have to solve with batching (DataLoader-style).
- Tooling and hosting are mature everywhere.

**When GraphQL actually pays off**

It earns its complexity when over-fetching and under-fetching are a measured problem, specifically:

- Your screens compose deeply nested, related data (user → posts → comments → authors), and with REST you'd either make many round-trips or build lots of custom "screen-shaped" endpoints.
- You have multiple client types (iOS, Android, web, maybe third parties) each wanting different fields from the same data, and you're tired of versioning endpoints for each.
- Mobile bandwidth is a genuine constraint and letting the client request exactly the fields it needs is worth the cost.

If those describe you, GraphQL's single flexible query per screen is a strong fit and the caching tradeoff is worth managing.

**The practical move:** unless you already know you're in that second bucket, build REST now. It won't trap you — you can put GraphQL in front of REST services later if the data-fetching pain materializes. Choosing GraphQL upfront, by contrast, commits you to its complexity before you know you need it.

One thing that would sharpen this: what does your data look like across screens — mostly flat lists and simple records, or deeply nested relationships? And is it one mobile client or several? If you tell me, I'll give you a firm answer instead of a default.
